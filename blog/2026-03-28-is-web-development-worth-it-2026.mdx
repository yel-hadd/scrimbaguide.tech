## What the BLS says about demand and pay

For **software developers** (BLS groups related QA/testing roles in the same profile), the latest published wage baseline remains in the **low-$130k median band** in national data ([BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook: Software Developers](https://www.bls.gov/ooh/Computer-and-Information-Technology/Software-developers.htm)). That is not an entry-level guarantee, it is a national median spanning experience levels.

For **job growth**, the same BLS profile projects developer demand as **much faster than the average** for all occupations ([BLS OOH, Software Developers](https://www.bls.gov/ooh/Computer-and-Information-Technology/Software-developers.htm)). In the prior projections cycle, BLS published higher figures for closely related developer categories in summary tables ([BLS Monthly Labor Review, 2023–33 projections overview](https://www.bls.gov/opub/mlr/2024/article/industry-and-occupational-employment-projections-overview-and-highlights-2023-33.htm)),headline rates shift when BLS rebases years, so compare the same vintage when debating trend threads.

**AI-adjacent roles** sit on an even hotter part of the curve in official projections: for example, **computer and information research scientists** are projected to grow far faster than baseline occupations ([BLS OOH: Computer and Information Research Scientists](https://www.bls.gov/ooh/Computer-and-Information-Technology/Computer-and-information-research-scientists.htm)), while total employment grows much more slowly in BLS projection summaries ([BLS employment projections summary](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/ecopro.nr0.htm)),a useful comparison when people say “only AI will matter” and imply web stacks vanish overnight.

### Interpreting “AI will take web dev”

Two facts can both be true:

1. **Copilots and agents** reduce time on boilerplate and search.
2. **Shipping** still requires architecture choices, edge cases, accessibility, performance budgets, and security instincts.

Many **AI engineer** job posts assume you can **build and iterate on web UIs and APIs**, not ignore them. Large annual surveys also show **high adoption of AI tools among professional developers** ([Stack Overflow Developer Survey, AI](https://survey.stackoverflow.co/2025/ai/)), which is less “replacement” and more “new default toolchain.”

On compensation, **role labels matter**: in the **Stack Overflow Developer Survey 2025** global sample, **AI/ML engineers** reported a **higher median total compensation** than many generalist developer titles in the same methodology ([Stack Overflow Developer Survey 2025, Work](https://survey.stackoverflow.co/2025/work/)). Some recent industry summaries claim developers who **routinely ship with AI-augmented workflows** out-earn peers by large margins in selected cohorts; those headlines mix **titles, regions, and experience**, so use them as **directional** motivation, not a personal guarantee, until you read the underlying methodology.

## What “worth it” should mean in 2026

1. **You can build** a small full-stack or frontend app **without** a video running.
2. **You can explain** tradeoffs (state, data fetching, deploy pipeline) in **five minutes**.
3. **You can show** three projects that are not identical to tutorial repos.

## Objection handling: “Isn’t the junior market brutal?”

It can be **competitive**. The counterweight is that **mediocre portfolios are common**. Narrow focus plus public artifacts still moves needles, especially when paired with consistent outreach and interview practice ([how to get hired with Scrimba](/blog/how-to-get-hired-with-scrimba) mirrors the same portfolio-first reality).

## Opportunity cost, framed honestly

If you spend **six months** passively watching long-form videos, the cost is not only time, it is **foregone reps**. The bootcamp price tag is scary partly because it is explicit; unpaid drift is still expensive.

A useful rule: **optimize for weekly “could I explain this on a whiteboard?” moments.** That metric correlates better with hiring than raw hour counts, and it is compatible with **low-cost monthly** tools or **$0** curricula alike.

## Geography and compensation reality

**BLS medians are national** and include experienced workers. New entrants should expect **lower starting offers** in many markets, with upside as they specialize. Remote hiring can widen access but rarely removes **proof-of-skill** requirements.

## Where AI helps (and where it does not)

**Helps:** boilerplate, refactors, test scaffolding, regex, SQL snippets, summarizing unfamiliar libraries.

**Hurts if you skip foundations:** you will not recognize **wrong** confident output without baseline literacy, especially around **async bugs**, **CORS**, and **auth**.

## Bootcamps vs subscriptions: the real differentiator

Bootcamps sell **accountability** and **cohort energy**. Subscriptions sell **iteration** and **low marginal cost** per extra month.

If you are highly self-directed, **free tracks plus disciplined building** can work. If you procrastinate unless someone schedules you, a bootcamp might still be rational even at high price, just model **loan payments** against realistic **time-to-first-offer** in your city.

## “Should I wait for the next hot framework?”

No. Employers still interview **problem decomposition**, **debugging**, and **delivery**. Frameworks churn; **shipping discipline** does not. Pick **one** mainstream stack, finish **three** projects, then revisit trends.
