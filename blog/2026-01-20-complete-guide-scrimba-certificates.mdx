# Scrimba Certificates: What They Actually Do (and Don't)

<DisclosureNotice />

If you're wondering whether a Scrimba certificate will get you hired, here's the short version: no certificate from any online course platform gets you hired by itself. Research on online credentials consistently shows that the issuing institution and the demonstrable skill matter far more than the certificate as an object. A Scrimba certificate is a useful "I finished this curriculum" signal on a LinkedIn profile, and the [Mozilla MDN partnership](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/blog/mdn-scrimba-partnership/) makes the Frontend path's certificate more credible than most. The certificate isn't the value, the path's curriculum is.

**Quick answer:** Scrimba awards a certificate when you complete 100% of a career path. It's verifiable (a public link shows your completion), it's shareable on LinkedIn, and it's not an accredited credential. Treat it as evidence you finished a curriculum, not as a credential that gets interviews on its own.

*Last reviewed: May 2026.*

## Who this is for

You're approaching the end of a Scrimba career path, or you're deciding whether to start one largely because of the certificate. You want a sober take on what it does in the job market.

## How you earn one

You need 100% completion of a designated [career path](/docs/paths/):

1. [Frontend Developer Path](/docs/paths/frontend-developer-path/) (81.6 hrs)
2. [Fullstack Developer Path](/docs/paths/fullstack-developer-path/) (108.4 hrs)
3. [Backend Developer Path](/docs/paths/backend-developer-path/) (39.4 hrs)
4. [AI Engineer Path](/docs/paths/ai-engineer-path/) (11.4 hrs)

Individual courses (Learn React, Learn JavaScript, etc.) don't issue certificates. Only paths do. That's intentional; it keeps the certificate tied to a meaningful body of work rather than 90 minutes of CSS Grid.

You need an active Pro subscription to be on a path in the first place. Free courses don't earn certificates.

> **One note on the AI Engineer Path certificate:** the path is the smallest of the four and is still expanding. New modules ship roughly quarterly. Starting now means your certificate covers the curriculum at its current scope; later subscribers earn one against a longer path. See the [AI Engineer Path overview](/docs/paths/ai-engineer-path/) for current modules, or compare path lengths in the [paths index](/docs/paths/).

## Adding it to LinkedIn

The process is the same as for any online certificate:

1. On your Scrimba dashboard, open the certificate and copy the public verification URL.
2. On LinkedIn, go to your profile, scroll to **Licenses & certifications**, click the plus button.
3. Fill in:
   - **Name**: "Frontend Developer Career Path" (or whichever path)
   - **Issuing organization**: Search "Scrimba" and pick the verified company page.
   - **Issue date**: Month and year you finished.
   - **Credential ID**: Leave blank or use the ID from the certificate URL.
   - **Credential URL**: Paste the public verification link.

Recruiters who click the link see the actual curriculum. That transparency is the certificate's main strength.

## Fixing common problems

**Name is wrong on the certificate.** It uses your Scrimba profile display name. Go to Settings, update your name, and the certificate regenerates. If it doesn't update, email help@scrimba.com with "Certificate name change" in the subject.

**You finished the path but don't see the certificate.** Open the path view and check for any lesson at less than 100%. Usually a one-minute outro or quiz at the end of a module hasn't been marked. Click into the path overview and complete any unchecked items.

**You want to download a PDF.** From the certificate page, there's a download option for a print-ready PDF.

## The honest take: does it help you get hired?

This is the question that actually matters, and the honest answer is: somewhat, in a specific way.

What research on online credentials says:

- Industry surveys on online credentials consistently find that the **reputation of the issuing institution** weighs heavily on how employers value a certificate. Scrimba isn't a university, but the Frontend path is formally endorsed by [MDN](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/blog/mdn-scrimba-partnership/), which adds credibility most online certificates lack.
- [Coursera's own research](https://www.coursera.org/articles/are-online-certificates-worth-it) on the value of online certificates emphasizes that **relevance of skills to the job** matters more than the credential itself. The certificate is a signal; the skills are the substance.
- HR-leader surveys repeatedly report that online credentials are roughly comparable to in-person credentials in perceived quality, with the issuing institution's reputation doing most of the heavy lifting.

What this means for Scrimba specifically:

- **Hiring managers at startups and small-to-mid tech companies** typically care about what you can do, not where you "studied." The certificate plus a working portfolio is a credible combination.
- **Hiring managers at large corporations with rigid HR filters** often filter by degrees first. A Scrimba certificate does not substitute for a degree in those pipelines.
- **The certificate is most useful** as one credible line on LinkedIn that explains your skills journey, especially if you're a career changer.

The MDN partnership is real. Scrimba's Frontend Developer Career Path was reviewed scrim-by-scrim by Mozilla's MDN team and aligned to the [MDN Curriculum](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/curriculum/). That's a credibility signal you don't get from most platform certificates. Even so, don't oversell it. Mozilla isn't issuing the certificate; Scrimba is. The MDN endorsement applies to the curriculum, not to a co-branded credential.

## What actually gets juniors hired in 2026

Based on what hiring engineers and bootcamp career-services teams consistently say:

1. **A working portfolio with at least one project that isn't a tutorial follow-along.** This is non-negotiable.
2. **A LinkedIn profile that doesn't read like a template.** Specific tech, specific projects.
3. **GitHub activity.** Real commits to your own projects, ideally one contribution to an open-source project.
4. **The ability to talk through your code in an interview.** Tutorial graduates often fail here.
5. **Hundreds, not dozens, of applications.** The 2026 junior market is competitive.

The certificate is line item zero on this list, useful but not load-bearing. See our [how to get hired](/blog/how-to-get-first-developer-job-2026/) guide and the [junior developer job market](/blog/junior-developer-job-market-2026/) reality check.

## The pragmatic recommendation

Take the certificate seriously enough to finish the path and add it to LinkedIn. Don't take it so seriously that you skip the work that actually gets you hired (portfolio, GitHub, applications). The path is the value; the certificate is the receipt.

If you haven't started a path yet, the [free courses](/blog/best-free-scrimba-courses/) are the right first step. Don't subscribe to chase a certificate; subscribe because you finished a free course and want the structured path.
